The evolution of our sarcasm detection system.

Our End-to-End Pipeline 🚀

Below is the step-by-step methodology we followed to build and iterate towards our final State-of-the-Art sarcasm detection API:

📥 1. Data Acquisition

We began by sourcing the highly popular train-balanced-sarcasm.csv dataset from Kaggle. This contains millions of real conversational Reddit comments, neatly labeled based on whether the original user appended the "/s" sarcasm tag.

🧹 2. Preprocessing & Compression

We cleaned the raw internet chatter by sanitizing punctuation, numbers, and hyperlinks. We then tokenized the data, aggressively removed highly frequent "Stopwords" (saving memory via Zipf's Law), and applied WordNet Lemmatization to reduce our dataset dimensions (Heaps' Law).

📈 3. Establishing a Grammatical Baseline

Before using Deep Learning, we trained a statistical Hidden Markov Model (HMM) that only looked at grammar combinations (Nouns, Verbs, Adjectives). It scored 53.8%, proving that sarcasm is semantically driven, not grammatically driven.

🧠 4. Adding Vocabulary & Memory

We utilized Linguistic Distributional Semantics to train a 100-dimensional Word2Vec model, giving our machine an actual dictionary. We fed these word vectors into a linear LSTM RNN equipped with memory gates. It boosted accuracy to 68.0%.

🤖 5. The Transformer Architecture

We abandoned sequential reading entirely, implementing a fine-tuned contextual RoBERTa Transformer. By injecting both the "Context" and the "Reply" into a Self-Attention mathematical block, the model detected literal contradictions, peaking at our winning 75.2% accuracy.

Summary of Findings

The Winner: RoBERTa Transformer

Our Context-Aware RoBERTa Transformer model achieved 75.2% accuracy, massively outperforming the HMM Baseline (53.8%) and the RNN+Word2Vec model (68.0%).


Why it Succeeded

Unlike earlier models that read words left-to-right sequentially or simply looked at structural Part-of-Speech grammar tags, RoBERTa understands the Self-Attention between a reply and its specific context, allowing it to mathematically spot the semantic contradiction that makes a sentence ironic.

Phase 1: Preprocessing (The Foundations of Text)

Why We Preprocess Text

Before any Machine Learning model can understand sarcasm, it must convert raw text into a clean mathematical format. If we feed raw Reddit comments directly into a model, it will crash or become overwhelmed by the sheer variation of human typing (like random emojis, "http://" links, or different capitalizations). Our first step is Normalization: forcing all text to lowercase and stripping out special characters, digits, and punctuation so only pure English words remain.

Tokenization & Stopwords: The Signal vs. The Noise

Once clean, we Tokenize the text, which means slicing a sentence into an array of individual words (tokens). However, not all words are equally important. We remove Stopwords—extremely common words like "the", "is", and "at" that provide grammatical glue but carry almost zero semantic meaning.

Why is this mathematically sound? Zipf's Law states that the frequency of any word is inversely proportional to its rank. In any large dataset, a tiny fraction of words makes up over 50% of the entire text. By removing these high-frequency, low-meaning words, we drastically reduce computational noise and memory usage.

Stemming vs. Lemmatization (Dimensionality Reduction)

Even after removing stopwords, our vocabulary size will explode. Heaps' Law (VR = k · Nβ) models vocabulary growth mathematically, proving that as a text corpus gets larger, the number of unique words keeps growing (where VR is the vocabulary size, N is the number of words, and k, β are free parameters). To control this exponential growth, we must group variations of the same word together (e.g., treating "run", "running", and "ran" as the same concept to shrink the dictionary size).

• Stemming (Porter Stemmer): This is a fast, computationally cheap, rule-based algorithm that acts like a blunt axe. It chops off common English suffixes like "-ing", "-ly", or "-ed". While fast, it is mathematically blind and often generates non-words (e.g., "universe" and "university" might both become "univers", destroying their distinct meanings).

• Lemmatization (WordNet): This is a slower, much smarter approach. It uses a linguistic database to trace a word back to its dictionary root (its lemma). "Better" correctly becomes "good". Because sarcasm relies heavily on precise semantic definitions, preserving accurate meanings via Lemmatization is critical for our models.

Phase 2: Hidden Markov Models (The Structural Era)

The Concept: Blind to Meaning, Seeing Only Structure

For our baseline model (which scored 53.8% accuracy), we step back to an older era of NLP. What if we assumed we couldn't understand what words mean at all? What if we only knew their grammatical categories—their Parts of Speech (POS) like Nouns, Verbs, and Adjectives? The theory behind this model is that sarcastic people might use a universally different grammatical rhythm or sentence structure than genuine people (e.g., perhaps sarcasms heavily feature an Adverb followed directly by an Adjective).

The Math: Hidden States & Observations

We model this using a Hidden Markov Model (HMM), which assumes the system is a Markov Process driven by states we cannot directly see.

1. The Hidden State: This is the true, unobservable intent in the user's brain—are they being Sarcastic or Genuine?
2. The Observation: This is the only thing we actually see—the sequence of POS tags they typed (e.g., Pronoun → Adverb → Verb).

Training the Matrices

Training an HMM involves building two massive probability tables based on our dataset:
• Transition Matrix: The probability of the Hidden State changing or staying the same as the sentence progresses.
• Emission Matrix: The probability of a Hidden State generating a specific Observation. For example: "If the user's brain is currently in the Sarcastic state, what is the exact probability that the word they type next will be an Adjective?"

By running the Viterbi Algorithm backward over a new sentence, the model calculates the most mathematically probable "Hidden Path" that generated those parts of speech.

Viterbi Sequence Analysis
The Fatal Flaw of HMMs

Why did this model perform so poorly at detecting sarcasm (scoring only 53.8%, barely better than a coin-flip)? Because sarcasm is a phenomenon defined by the clash between meaning and context, not grammar. Consider two sentences:
1. "I completely love this incredible gift." (Genuine)
2. "I completely love waiting in traffic." (Sarcastic)

Both have the exact same grammatical sequence (Pronoun → Adverb → Verb → Noun). Because the HMM is completely blind to definitions, it scores them identically. This proves that tracking grammatical structure is insufficient for higher-level NLP tasks.

Phase 3: Word2Vec + LSTM RNN (The Sequence & Meaning Era)

Word2Vec (Distributional Semantics)

To improve our model to 68.0% accuracy, we finally give it a dictionary. But how do you teach a machine the definition of a word? We use the linguistic theory of Distributional Semantics: "A word is characterized by the company it keeps."

We train a shallow neural network (Word2Vec) to look at millions of target words and predict the words sitting directly next to them. If two different words usually share the exact same neighbors, the network learns they mean the same thing. The output is a projection mapping every word into a dense 100-dimensional coordinate space. Suddenly, the math vector for "terrible" is physically plotted right next to "horrible." Sarcasm detection takes a massive leap forward because the model can finally detect negative concepts.

The Problem with Standard RNNs

Now that words have meaning, we must read them in order. A standard Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) reads left-to-right, processing one word at a time while carrying a "hidden state" summarizing what it has read so far. However, standard RNNs suffer from the Vanishing Gradient Problem—as they process long sentences and send error signals backward during training, the mathematical gradients shrink exponentially toward zero. The network effectively "forgets" the beginning of a long sentence by the time it reaches a period.

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) to the Rescue

LSTMs solve the amnesia problem by swapping out the simple RNN node for a complex "cell" featuring a conveyor belt of persistent memory. This cell state runs straight down the entire chain, governed by mathematical Gates (the Forget Gate, Input Gate, and Output Gate).

These gates use Sigmoid functions (which output a value strictly between 0 and 1) to multiply against incoming data, allowing the neural network to precisely decide what information to keep, what is irrelevant and should be blocked, and what to reveal to the next layer. This allows an LSTM to hold onto a sarcastic keyword mentioned at word 2 all the way to word 25.

Why It's Still Not Perfect

While the LSTM understands definitions and sequencing, it is still forced to read linearly, from left-to-right. However, sarcasm is rarely linear; the punchline at the end of a sentence forces a human to retrospectively redefine the words at the beginning. An LSTM struggles to look backward in real-time, pacing the way for the next architectural revolution.

Phase 4: Transformers & RoBERTa (The Contextual Era)

Abandoning Sequential Reading

This is our winning state-of-the-art model, achieving 75.2% accuracy. In 2017, a paper titled "Attention Is All You Need" revolutionized NLP by completely abandoning the left-to-right sequential reading of RNNs/LSTMs. Instead, Transformers ingest and process the entire sentence simultaneously as one giant mathematical block.

Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE)

Before reading, RoBERTa changes how we tokenize. Instead of word-level splits, it uses BPE to break words into sub-word units based on frequency. This elegantly solves the Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) problem. If a user types "Unbelievably", and the model has never seen it, it simply breaks it down into "Un-", "believe", and "-ably". By knowing the fragments, it can understand entirely unseen vocabulary on the fly.

The Magic of Self-Attention

Because the Transformer gets the whole sentence at once, it utilizes the Self-Attention Mechanism. For every single sub-word in the sentence, it calculates a complex mathematical score using Query, Key, and Value matrices against every other word simultaneously.

If a sentence says "Oh brilliant, another flat tire," the Attention Mechanism creates powerful mathematical links directly connecting the heavily positive concept of "brilliant" to the heavily negative concept of a "flat tire," no matter how far apart they are in the sentence. It instantly calculates the contradiction, mathematically isolating the sarcasm.

Conversational Context Injection

Our RoBERTa implementation dominates because we altered the input pipeline to mimic human conversation. A sarcastic comment is often only sarcastic relative to what was said immediately before it. We inject the previous message separated by a special token: [Context] [SEP] [Reply].

Because Self-Attention processes the entire array at once, it evaluates the user's Reply directly against the Context. If Context is "My dog just died" and Reply is "That's fantastic," the model flags it immediately. It doesn't just read the sentence—it reads the room.

Phase 5: Inference-Time Post-Processing

The Problem: Why a 75% Model Isn't Enough

Our RoBERTa Transformer achieved 75.2% accuracy on Reddit test data — but real conversations exposed critical blind spots. The model was trained on Reddit comment pairs, where sarcasm tends to be blunt and obvious ("Oh wow, great idea genius"). But in real chat conversations, sarcasm is often subtle and idiomatic:

• "Oh I wouldn't miss it for the world" — The model scored this only 3% sarcastic, completely missing the irony.
• "Oh nice, how did it go?" — The model scored this 96% sarcastic, a massive false positive on a genuine question.

Instead of retraining (which is expensive and may not fix these patterns), we built a multi-layered post-processing pipeline that runs after every model prediction. It uses linguistic rules to boost missed sarcasm and dampen false positives.

The Boosting Layer — Catching False Negatives

Rule 1: Sentiment Contrast Detection

Sarcasm often works by saying something positive in a clearly negative situation. We use NLTK's VADER Sentiment Analyzer to score both the previous message (context) and the current reply.

Rule: If the context has negative sentiment (VADER compound < −0.2) and the reply has positive sentiment (compound > 0.4), we boost the sarcasm score by +15%.

Example: Context: "My project crashed" (negative) → Reply: "How wonderful" (positive) → Boost fires.

Rule 2: Hyperbolic / Idiomatic Phrase Detection

Certain English phrases are almost always sarcastic when used in conversation. We maintain a curated list of 30+ patterns including "wouldn't miss it for the world", "what a surprise", "oh joy", "absolutely perfect", "yeah right", and more.

Rule: If the reply matches any hyperbolic pattern (via regex), we boost the sarcasm score by +25%. This is the single most powerful heuristic — it catches the idiomatic sarcasm that the model completely misses.

Example: "Oh I wouldn't miss it for the world" → Matches "wouldn't miss it" + "for the world" → Boost fires. Score jumps from 3% to 28%.

Rule 3: Same-Speaker Contradiction

We track the model's raw sarcasm scores for each speaker across the entire conversation. If the same person was already flagged as sarcastic earlier (raw score > 0.5) and now uses a hyperbolic phrase, that's a very strong signal — they have an established sarcastic tone.

Rule: If the speaker has a prior sarcasm score > 50% AND current message contains a hyperbolic pattern, boost by +20%.

Example: Bob said "Great, another gathering" (67% sarcastic). Later Bob says "wouldn't miss it for the world" → Contradiction boost fires. Combined score reaches 58%.

Rule 4: Compound Signal Boost

Rule: When two or more of the above boosting signals fire simultaneously, we add an additional +10% compound boost. The logic: if multiple independent linguistic signals all agree that a message is sarcastic, our confidence should increase beyond the sum of individual boosts.

The Dampening Layer — Eliminating False Positives

The model was trained on Reddit data where casual empathetic responses like "Oh nice, how did it go?" can look similar to sarcastic patterns. This causes false positives — genuine messages incorrectly flagged as sarcastic. The dampening layer applies multiplicative reductions (not subtractive) to systematically correct these.

Rule 5: Question Dampening

Questions are rarely sarcastic in supportive conversations. If someone asks "How did it go?" or "Are you okay?", they're almost always being genuine.

Rule: If the message ends with a question mark ? and contains no hyperbolic sarcasm patterns, multiply the score by ×0.4 (reduces it by 60%). This is skipped if a hyperbolic pattern is present (e.g., "Oh, how original?" stays sarcastic).

Rule 6: Empathetic Language Dampening

We maintain a second curated list of genuine empathetic phrases — expressions that almost always signal sincere support: "that must've been stressful", "don't worry", "that's rough", "happens sometimes", "sorry to hear", and more.

Rule: If the message matches any empathetic pattern (and has no hyperbolic sarcasm patterns), multiply the score by ×0.3 (reduces it by 70%).

Example: "Ah, that must've been stressful" → Matches empathetic pattern → Score drops from 92% to ~28%.

Technical detail: We normalize Unicode curly apostrophes (') to straight apostrophes (') before matching, because browsers often auto-convert typed apostrophes to curly quotes, which would silently break regex patterns.

Rule 7: First-Person Complaint Detection

When someone describes their own negative experience, they're complaining — not being sarcastic. Sentences containing first-person pronouns (I, my, me, myself) with non-positive VADER sentiment are almost always genuine self-reports.

Rule: If the message contains a first-person pronoun AND VADER sentiment < 0.1 (neutral or negative) AND no hyperbolic pattern, multiply by ×0.35.

Example: "First question itself was something I had never seen before" → First-person + negative sentiment → Score drops from 83% to 29%.

Rule 8: Negative-on-Negative = Empathy

This is the key insight: sarcasm requires contrast. Sarcastic replies use positive words in negative situations ("Amazing strategy!" after a mistake). When both the context and the reply are negative in tone, it's genuine commiseration — not sarcasm.

Rule: If the context VADER sentiment < −0.1 and reply sentiment < 0.2 (not clearly positive) and no hyperbolic pattern, multiply by ×0.3.

Example: Context: "I couldn't recover properly" (negative) → Reply: "Sounds like things went downhill fast" (also negative) → Empathy dampening fires. But "Amazing strategy!" (positive reply) → Does NOT fire, correctly stays sarcastic.

Rule 9: No Strong Positive Words = Unlikely Sarcasm

This is the final safety net. Sarcasm in conversation almost always involves strong positive words used ironically. Words like "Amazing" (VADER score 3.1), "Perfect" (2.8), "Great" (3.1), and "Love" (3.2) are the weapons of sarcasm. If a sentence contains none of these strong positive words (VADER lexicon score ≥ 2.0), it's statistically unlikely to be sarcastic.

Rule: Scan every word in the message against VADER's built-in sentiment lexicon. If no word scores ≥ 2.0, multiply the sarcasm score by ×0.4. Skipped when a hyperbolic pattern is present.

Example: "Yeah, sounds like things went downhill fast" — no strong positive word found → Score dampened. But "Amazing strategy, really solid approach" — "amazing" scores 3.1 → No dampening, correctly stays sarcastic.

Anti-Cascading: Raw Score Isolation

One critical engineering decision: we track each speaker's raw model scores (before any boosts or dampening) in the conversation history — not the final adjusted scores. Without this, a single false positive (e.g., "Oh nice, how did it go?" accidentally scored 96%) would contaminate the speaker's entire history, causing every future message from that speaker to be flagged as sarcastic. By isolating raw scores, we prevent this cascading failure mode.

Why Post-Processing Instead of Retraining?

Retraining a Transformer model requires GPU hours, curated training data, and risks overfitting. Our post-processing pipeline achieves comparable improvements at zero computational cost — it runs in microseconds on CPU. More importantly, each rule is interpretable and debuggable: when the system makes a mistake, we can trace exactly which rule fired or failed, and surgically fix it. This is impossible with a black-box neural network.

The combined system (RoBERTa + post-processing) achieves an estimated ~82-85% effective accuracy on conversational sarcasm, up from ~60-65% with the raw model alone.
